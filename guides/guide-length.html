1. Why unit choice matters

Units communicate scale. Choosing millimetres for a room will produce awkward large numbers; choosing kilometres for a bench measurement will hide needed precision. Use:

  • mm/cm ‚Äî for small or precision tasks (electronics, carpentry, recipes).
  • m ‚Äî for rooms, buildings, most practical measurements.
  • km/mi ‚Äî for travel distances and maps.

Keeping units consistent reduces errors and unnecessary rounding.

2. Exact constants you must memorise

  • 1 m = 100 cm
  • 1 in = 2.54 cm (exact by international agreement)
  • 1 ft = 12 in = 0.3048 m (exact)
  • 1 mi = 1609.344 m

5. Choosing precision & rounding rules

Decide precision based on the task:

  • Rough: 0‚Äì1 significant figures (landscape area)
  • Home DIY: nearest 1‚Äì5 mm or nearest 1/8 inch for joinery
  • Engineering: use the specification tolerance (often ¬±0.01 mm)

When converting, keep a consistent number of decimal places across steps to avoid false precision.

6. Common pitfalls & how to avoid them

  • Avoid mixing imperial/metric without converting first.
  • Watch unit prefixes: 1 mm = 0.001 m (not 0.1 m).
  • Don't round mid-calculation ‚Äî round only the final result to the required precision.

7. Practical measurement tips

  • Measure twice and record in the same unit.
  • Use the same tool across measurements for consistency (same tape, same zero reference).
  • For long distances prefer laser rangefinders or odometer/ GPS ‚Äî record decimals consistently (e.g., 2.34 m).

8. Programming & calculator snippets
